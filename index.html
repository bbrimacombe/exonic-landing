The largest revolution in history is afoot.

Artificial intelligence will soon unlock the source code of life.

For millennia, great minds have fantasized of curing all diseases and discovering eternal life.

This is now only years away.

July, 2021. DeepMind’s AlphaFold 2 was used to predict structures for over 200 million proteins – covering nearly all known proteins on Earth – expanding the structural biology dataset by over 1,000×.

November, 2024. The Evo model was trained on 2.7 million prokaryotic and phage genomes. It demonstrated zero-shot functional prediction and successfully generated novel CRISPR-Cas systems, transposons, and de novo protein-RNA constructs.

December, 2024. Evo 1.5 was prompted to generate anti-CRISPRs from CRISPR, and anti-toxins from toxins. In wet-lab tests, 50% of anti-toxin candidates neutralized toxins in E. coli.

February, 2025. Evo 2 scaled to 9.3 trillion base pairs of training data from 128,000 species. Evo 2 generates full synthetic genomes with functional open reading frames and regulatory structure.

GPT-4o was trained on around 10 trillion tokens — most of the text ever written by humanity.

Biological data exists on an unfathomably larger scale.

Each human genome contains 3.2 billion base pairs of DNA. Across 8 billion people, that’s 2.5 x 10^28 base pairs.

A single human cell contains 10 million RNA molecules. With trillions of cells per person, the number of unique RNA transcripts across humanity exceeds 10^32.

The number of unique proteins exceeds 1 million per human individual. Across humanity, that’s 10^15 distinct proteoforms.

This is the tip of the iceberg.

Earth is home to an estimated 8.7 million eukaryotic species — and potentially over 1 trillion microbial species.

There are an estimated 10^31 viruses on Earth — more than stars in the observable universe.

...An so on. In short, the amount of data available for biological foundation models is unlimited.

So now what?

These models are so new, that the industry is only beginning to grasp their implications — let alone productize them. As of the time of writing, Evo 2 has only 22 citations. There are no drugs on the market designed by biological foundation models.

Companies like Profluent, Inceptive, and Cradle are building with these models — but efforts are closed, preclinical, and exploratory.

And herein lies the thesis of Exonic AI.

No one can predict what will happen. But we know that something will happen.

How do we bet on something?

More than 2 million ordinary people have contributed to citizen science efforts like Foldit and Folding@Home, helping solve protein structures and simulate molecular dynamics from their personal devices.

How do we bet on human curiosity and inginuity?

Exonic AI will tap into human curiosity and build the app store of biological foundation models.

Master plan

1. Make bio foundation models easy, fun, and accessible to everyone.
- No PhD required. Simple, minimalist, fun UI
- Accessible via web & mobile
- Gamify: leaderboard and open sharing
- Real-world utility: let users submit designs to wet labs for validation with the click of a button
- “Help cure cancer from your couch”
- Monetization via $19.99/mo, $250–500 wet-lab sponsorships, and premium tools. 10,000 paying users is > $2.4 M ARR

2. Move upstream to researchers
- Build on the open community by offering next-gen bioinformatics infrastructure
- Give researchers fine-grained control: embeddings, mutational scanning, genome-wide modeling
- Allow labs to bring their own data, fine-tune foundation models, or deploy private versions
- Monetize via SaaS research licenses, enterprise API tiers, and institutional partnerships

3. Drug discovery
- Surface the best user and researcher-generated sequences for downstream development
- Partner with biotech firms or spin out internal programs targeting high-confidence molecules
- Generate and license IP around novel proteins, antibodies, RNA therapeutics, and more
- At scale, this becomes not just a platform — but a new kind of pharmaceutical company powered by open resources and continuous discovery
